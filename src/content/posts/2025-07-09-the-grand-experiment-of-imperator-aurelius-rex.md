The Grand Experiment of Imperator Aurelius Rex
For illphated.com

In the flickering neon haze of New Rome—a towering megacity in what was once the American Midwest—one man stood above the skyline, draped in ancient robes and future metals. He was Imperator Aurelius Rex, a Roman emperor reborn in the Blade Runner era, bred not from blood but from the algorithmic convergence of civilization’s most powerful archetypes: Caesar, Washington, and a quantum AI named G.O.D.S.P.E.E.D.

Behind him, the stars and stripes still waved—glitching slightly in the low-orbit light pollution—but it remained. A battered relic of an old world, projected proudly above the cybercolosseum he had built for The Experiment.

And what was The Experiment?

Aurelius Rex called it Civitas Nova: a sprawling society of genetically and ideologically diverse citizens randomly assigned roles. Philosophers were made janitors. Gang leaders were turned into judges. The poor were given limitless credits, and the rich were forced to survive a week on ration chips and surveillance gigs.

No one was allowed to question it. The test tubes were metaphorical, the variables vast. The city was his petri dish.

Every week, a new set of rules. Every month, a new hypothesis. Could virtue be manufactured? Could justice evolve? Could freedom withstand engineered chaos?

The world watched. Some cheered. Others warned. But Aurelius Rex had no need for approval. “The American dream,” he said with a half-smirk beneath his laurel-wreathed helmet, “was always a social experiment. I’m just running it properly.”

And in the humid purple glow of perpetual dusk, with drones buzzing like flies around the Colossus of Liberty, he raised a glass tube to the skyline and whispered,
“Let the truth be cultured.”
