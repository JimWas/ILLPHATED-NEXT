Everyone Has the Right to Vote—But That Doesn’t Mean Your Vote Gets Counted
In the smoky, neon-lit shadows of a cyberpunk dystopia, a lone armored figure sits in front of a retro-futuristic terminal. The screen glows with one hard truth:

"Everyone has the right to vote according to the Constitution—
But the Constitution doesn’t say that everybody’s vote has to be counted."

This haunting visual, straight from the Blade Runner universe with vaporwave colors bleeding across the skyline, raises a fundamental question:
Is the right to vote truly meaningful if that vote can be ignored?

The Constitutional Loophole No One Talks About
In the United States, the Constitution guarantees the right to vote through a patchwork of amendments—the 15th, 19th, 24th, and 26th—but nowhere does it explicitly guarantee that every legally cast vote must be counted. That gray area leaves room for mischief: purges, suppression, gerrymandering, and "lost" ballots.

Why This Matters in 2025
In an age of AI, misinformation, and algorithmic manipulation, vote integrity is more vital—and more vulnerable—than ever. From deepfakes influencing public opinion to targeted disinformation campaigns and restricted access to polling stations, your vote may be your last honest signal in a world of noise.

But if it’s never counted… what does it matter?
